Chapter 03 · The evidence

Three peer-reviewed sources
anchoring this project.

Each card connects an empirical finding to the anthropological argument: digital environments are now a primary force shaping how humans think, behave, and form culture.

Frontiers in Psychology · 2023

Xie et al.: Short-form video addiction & academic procrastination

A moderated mediation model showing how attentional control links short-form media use to declining academic performance among undergraduates.

→ Frequent users prefer immediate rewards, weakening delayed gratification, a measurable behavioral shift.
Frontiers in Psychology · 2024

Yan et al.: Mobile short-video use & executive function

Experimental and observational data showing decreased self-control and impaired executive attention with frequent short-form exposure.

→ Habitual task-switching weakens the brain's ability to engage in deep, sustained thinking.
Computers in Human Behavior · 2025

Nguyen et al.: Feeds, feelings, and focus (meta-analysis)

Synthesizes findings across studies: reduced attention span, lower cognitive performance, and elevated anxiety from heavy short-form use.

→ Effects are widespread and consistent, evidence of a broad cultural shift in how information is consumed.
Anthropological Lens

Why this matters for cultural anthropology

Short-form platforms aren't neutral tools. They're cultural environments. The patterns above reveal how humans adapt cognitively and socially to technologies designed for engagement, not understanding.

→ Culture is no longer just transmitted through ritual or kinship. It's increasingly transmitted through algorithmic feeds.
